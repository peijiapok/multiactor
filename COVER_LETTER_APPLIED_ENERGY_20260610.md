Dear Editor,

Please consider our manuscript, “Multi-actor acceptability of EV fleet charging control under capacity and grid constraints,” for publication in Applied Energy.

The manuscript addresses a deployment problem in managed electric-vehicle charging: policies that appear effective under single metrics such as delivered energy, cost, queue activity, or peak demand may not be acceptable simultaneously to drivers, fleet or facility operators, and grid operators. We develop a multi-actor acceptability-gate framework and evaluate EV fleet charging control under capacity limits, behavior stress tests, grid-policy interventions, and a representative IEEE-33 feeder stress screen.

The main contribution is an energy-systems evaluation framework rather than a controller-superiority claim. In the tested weekly severity sweep, service-oriented charging passes all actor gates under full compliance, while severity-1 behavior removes same-episode all-pass outcomes under the specified gates. A targeted 28-day check at 35% capacity supports this transition. A demanded-kWh trace shows that the behavioral effect is request-event churn rather than increased simulator demanded kWh. The study also shows that PeakPenalty can reduce selected peak metrics without rescuing driver/fleet acceptability failures, and that IEEE-33 feeder results should be interpreted as EV-off-relative stress deltas rather than site-specific validation.

We believe the manuscript fits Applied Energy because it connects EV charging control, behavioral response risk, and distribution-grid stress screening into a reproducible applied energy-system evaluation. The manuscript avoids unsupported claims of optimality, field-calibrated behavior, or feeder validation, and includes explicit limitations and reproducibility boundaries.
